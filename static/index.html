1) Time Series Analysis — Energy Consumption Over Time

Interpretation: The daily average electricity consumption demonstrates high variability and clear cyclical trends over the two-year period (Dec 2006 – Dec 2008). You can observe short-term fluctuations (weekly cycles) alongside a broader wave representing the long-term seasonal pattern.

2) Distribution Analysis — Household Power Consumption

Interpretation: The energy consumption distribution is right-skewed. Most days exhibit a mean consumption level concentrated between 0.5 kW and 2.0 kW, representing standard daily household baseline activities. Extreme values stretching above 2.5 kW represent high-load days, typically corresponding to intense winter heating demands.

3) Correlation Analysis — Heatmap of Variables

Interpretation: The heatmap highlights the pairwise linear relationships across the features:
  • Global_active_power is almost perfectly correlated with Global_intensity (r ≈ 1.00), representing the physical relationship between current and power load.
  • Negative correlations with weather features like tavg and tmax highlight the seasonal effect where rising temperatures reduce household heating demand.
  • Pairwise feature collinearities above 0.90 (e.g. tmin, tmax, Month) are filtered out during training to keep the model numerically stable.

4) Seasonal Trend Analysis — Season-Wise Usage

Interpretation: Season-wise averages confirm that Winter (Dec–Feb) is the peak energy consumption period (~1.5+ kW daily mean) due to heating and longer lighting hours. Summer (Jun–Aug) shows the lowest baseline energy usage (~0.9 kW daily mean) as heating is turned off and daylight hours are maximized, followed by transitional seasons (Spring and Autumn).

🧠 How Accuracy & Metrics are Calculated

The performance and prediction correctness of each machine learning model are calculated on the test set using the following standard statistical regression metrics:

  1. Prediction Accuracy (%):

    Calculated as the complement of the Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE). This expresses how close the predictions are to the actual values on a 0–100% scale (where 100% is a perfect forecast):

  2. R² Score (Coefficient of Determination):

    Measures the proportion of variance in electricity consumption captured and explained by the model features. An R² of 1.0 (100%) means the model perfectly explains all consumption fluctuations:

  3. Mean Absolute Error (MAE):

    The average absolute difference between the actual readings and predicted values. It tells us, on average, how many kilowatts (kW) our forecasts are off by:
